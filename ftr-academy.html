Chapter 1 Why Marcus needs a group — not just a course Done ✓ ↓

Marcus was promoted because he was the best individual performer. He got to his level alone. That's the exact pattern that will break him as a leader — and the same pattern that makes a self-paced video course the wrong first product for him. He needs to be in a room where other people like him show up with the same blind spots. That's the cohort's unfair advantage.

Why group beats solo for Marcus
  • He identifies with peers, not teachers. Marcus doesn't take advice easily — he's a Captain. But when someone with the same title and the same pain says "I had that exact problem," it lands. Put 10 Marcuses in a room and they coach each other.
  • He needs evidence, not ideas. Marcus runs on data. Seeing other sales managers implement a coaching session structure and report back with real numbers next week is more convincing than any framework slide you can build.
  • Accountability is built-in. A video course gives Marcus an escape — he can pause it when a deal comes in. In the cohort he committed to 10 other people. The social cost of not showing up is real.
  • The cohort is F.E.E.L. in practice. Every session models the exact behavior you're teaching. You run a check-in (Feel). You ask questions instead of giving answers (Explore). You create hot seat moments (Elevate). You close with commitments (Lead). They learn by experiencing it, not reading it.
  • 90%+ completion vs. 3–5%. Self-paced courses have a 3–5% completion rate. Cohort-based programs hit 90%+. Marcus finishes what he starts when there's a social contract around it.

The core insight: "Det der gjorde dig til den bedste sælger er præcis det der gør dig til en dårlig leder." That sentence is the cohort's reason for existing. Marcus built his career by going it alone. The cohort is the first time he does something hard in a group — and sees that it's faster.

Chapter 2 Designing the 6-session F.E.E.L. curriculum Done ✓ ↓

3 months. 6 sessions. 90 minutes each. One letter of F.E.E.L. per session — with Session 1 as orientation and Session 6 as integration. Each session teaches one concept, creates one breakthrough, and sends Marcus home with one experiment to run on his team before the next session.

6-session arc
  • Session 1 — Orientation: Who's in the room. The 10 pains. Naming Point A honestly. Establish peer pairs. Contract: this only works if you show up as you actually are — not as you think a manager should be.
  • Session 2 — F (Foundation): Lead yourself first. The whole person: sleep, movement, nutrition, emotional regulation. The Energy Compass. "You can't give energy you don't have." Participants bring their Energy Log from the week. Hot seat: one person unpacks a moment where their energy derailed something.
  • Session 3 — E (Elevation): Feel The Room. Your nervous system is contagious — for good and bad. Polyvagal Theory in practice: how to read the room before you speak. Body language, HeartMath, state management. A regulated nervous system is the prerequisite for reading people. Marcus learns to sense what's happening in a room — not just react to it.
  • Session 4 — E (Exploration): Lead With Curiosity. The question is more powerful than the answer. Dream→Current→Problem→Probe. Practice the 7 coaching questions on each other in pairs. Debrief: what did it feel like to be asked instead of directed? Marcus becomes a leader who unlocks — not one who instructs.
  • Session 5 — L (Liberation): Performative Balance. Culture is not a poster — it's what happens when you're not in the room. Psychological safety (Edmondson). Radical Candor. Belonging Cues (Coyle). Recruitment through the F.E.E.L. lens: see the whole person, not just the CV. Build a team that performs because they want to.
  • Session 6 — Integration: Each participant presents one thing that changed in their team over 3 months. Real metrics. Real stories. You facilitate, you don't teach. They've become each other's evidence. Close: the path forward — course, 1:1, or next cohort round.
Action step

Build a one-page curriculum doc (PDF or Notion) that you share with applicants before they enroll. One line per session: the concept, the exercise, and the one experiment they'll run before the next session. This document sells the cohort as much as any landing page does.

Chapter 3 Running a live session — facilitation the F.E.E.L. way Done ✓ ↓

Every session you facilitate is a live demonstration of F.E.E.L. You're not just teaching it — you're embodying it. If you walk in with your own agenda and talk for 90 minutes, Marcus will clock it. He's been in those meetings. He's been the one running them. Your job is to be the opposite of that.

90-minute session structure
  • 0–10 min — Check-in (Feel): One question, everyone answers. "What's one word for the energy you're bringing in today?" Gets people present. If someone says "distracted" — that's data. Acknowledge it. Don't rush past it.
  • 10–35 min — Teaching (Explore): One concept only. No more. Use a story from your own experience first — a moment where you had this problem. Then the framework. If you're talking for 25 minutes straight, you've stopped facilitating and started lecturing.
  • 35–60 min — Application (Elevate): Breakout rooms, 2–3 people. Give them a specific prompt tied to the teaching. "Tell each other about a conversation last week where you gave an answer when you should have asked a question." Debrief as a group.
  • 60–80 min — Hot seat (Lead): One participant volunteers a real challenge. You coach them live. The group watches. This is where F.E.E.L. becomes tangible — everyone sees the methodology in real time. Use the 7 coaching questions. Don't solve it for them.
  • 80–90 min — Close: Each person states one commitment before the next session. Specific: what, when, and how they'll know they did it. These commitments get shared in the group chat — accountability starts before they log off.

Energy reset before every session: Don't walk straight from a difficult call into the cohort. Give yourself 5 minutes. Reset. The room will read whatever energy you bring in — and they're paying $3,500 to be in the best version of that room.

Action step

Write your Session 2 (Foundation) run-of-show before Cohort 1 starts. Exact check-in question. The one story you'll open with. The teaching concept in 3 bullet points max. The breakout prompt. The hot seat setup. Practice it out loud — alone — the day before. Not to be perfect. To find where you'll lose the thread.

Chapter 4 Between sessions — the community layer Done ✓ ↓

90% of the learning happens between sessions — when Marcus tries something on his team, it doesn't go the way he expected, and he has to figure out why. If the cohort only exists in the 90 minutes on Zoom, you're leaving most of the value on the table. The group chat is where the real work happens.

Between-session infrastructure
  • Peer pairs from Day 1: Pair participants at the orientation session. They check in once a week — 15 minutes each. Their job: "What did you try? What happened?" It costs you nothing and dramatically increases retention and completion.
  • Experiment sharing in the group: Every session ends with one experiment to run before the next session. The group chat is where they report back mid-week. Seed it yourself — share a reflection from your own week first.
  • "Win of the week" thread: Every Monday, everyone shares one thing that went well with their team. It builds momentum, creates social proof within the cohort, and keeps Marcus engaged when his week gets busy.
  • One "office hours" slot per month: Optional. 30 minutes. Unstructured. Come with a question or don't come at all. The people who show up have the most specific, high-quality questions — and they become your most engaged alumni.
  • Experiments, not homework: Don't send readings. Send doing. "Before we meet again: use the Dream→Current→Problem flow in one conversation with a rep. Notice what happens. Come ready to talk about it." A doing assignment has a 3x higher completion rate than a reading.
Action step

Chapter 1 First call — mapping Point A with the F.E.E.L. flow Done ✓ ↓

The first session is the most important one. It sets the emotional contract, surfaces the real problem (which is rarely the one they say out loud on the discovery call), and establishes you as someone who listens before they speak. Done well, the client leaves the first session already thinking differently. Done poorly, they wonder if this was worth $4,500.

Session 1 structure (60 min)
  • 0–10 min — Why are they here? "Tell me what made you say yes to this." Let them talk. Don't interrupt. You're listening for the real Point A — the one underneath the one they're saying. A Captain will say "I want to be a better leader." What he means is "my team doesn't trust me and I don't know why."
  • 10–30 min — Point A (Current State): What's working. What isn't. What they're not willing to say yet. Use the Explore questions: "Tell me more." Never "why." "What's the impact of that on your team?" "What does it cost you when this happens?" Let them feel the gap — don't create it for them.
  • 30–50 min — Point B (Dream): "What does success look like 6 months from now — specifically?" Not feelings. Evidence. "My team's eNPS goes from 40 to 70." "I stop having the same performance conversation with the same person every month." "I walk into Monday without dread." Specific Point B = the spine of the whole engagement.
  • 50–58 min — Contract: Agree on 2–3 focus themes. Not 10 — 2 or 3. These become the recurring lens for every session. Write them down together. "We're going to spend the next 6 months on: coaching instead of directing, energy management under pressure, and the one team member you're avoiding."
  • 58–60 min — Logistics: Communication between sessions (voice note? Email? WhatsApp?), what to prepare before each call, rescheduling policy. Keep it simple. Make it feel effortless to be your client.

Chapter 2 The recurring session — 60 minutes with the F.E.E.L. arc Done ✓ ↓

Sessions 2 through 6 (or 12) follow the same rhythm. Consistency is trust. Your client knows what to expect — and can prepare accordingly. The structure is simple: show up, check in, close the loop from last time, do the work, synthesise, commit. Repeat until Point B is real.

Recurring session structure (60 min)
  • 0–5 min — Check-in: "What's your energy today? What's on your mind that needs to get out of the way before we start?" A Captain will say "fine" and want to get to business. Push gently: "And what's the one thing you haven't told anyone yet this week?" That's where the real session starts.
  • 5–15 min — Accountability: "What did you commit to last time?" "What actually happened?" No judgment. Pure curiosity. "What did you learn from that?" If they didn't do it — explore why. That gap is usually the coaching.
  • 15–45 min — Core work: One challenge, one pattern, one insight. Ask more than you tell. Use the 7 coaching questions. When you feel the urge to give advice — ask instead. "What do you think is stopping you?" is always more powerful than your answer.
  • 45–55 min — Synthesis: "What was the most important insight from today?" Let them say it — not you. They name it, they own it. If they can't name it, you haven't done enough Explore work.
  • 55–60 min — Commitment: One specific action before the next session. What. When. How they'll know they did it. This is the "Lead" step — they close their own session.

The ask vs. tell ratio: Track how often you speak vs. how often you ask. If you're speaking more than 30% of the time, you're consulting, not coaching. The goal: ask a question, let them answer fully, then ask the next question. Your value is in the questions — not the answers.

Chapter 3 The 6-month vs. 12-month arc — what changes each quarter Done ✓ ↓

One session per month is enough. Marcus will push back — "isn't that too infrequent?" The answer: no. Because between sessions he's doing the work. He's leading his team. He's running the experiments. He's failing and learning. Your 60 minutes is the container that makes sense of all of that. Here's what the arc looks like across 6 and 12 months.

6-month arc ($4,500)
  • Month 1 — Discovery: Deep listening. Establish trust. Map Point A fully. Don't rush to solutions. The patterns that need changing rarely show up in the first session — they show up in month 2.
  • Months 2–3 — Pattern identification: Spot the 1–2 behaviours that are holding him back. Name them together. "I notice that every time your team pushes back, you give them the answer instead of letting them find it. What do you think is behind that?"
  • Months 4–5 — Behavioural experiments: He tries new things. You debrief what happened. This is where the F.E.E.L. methodology moves from theory to muscle memory.
  • Month 6 — Consolidation: What changed? What's the evidence? What does Marcus want to keep working on? Plant the seed for a 12-month extension or cohort participation. Don't make it a hard close — make it a natural conversation.
12-month arc ($7,500)
  • Q1 — Same as 6-month months 1–2. Discovery, trust, first patterns emerging.
  • Q2 — Identity work. Marcus has been running on his sælger-identity for years. In Q2, you go deeper: "Who are you as a leader — separate from your results?" This is where the PI profile and Social Needs lens become tools. A Captain needs Power — how does he meet that need through his team instead of over them?
  • Q3 — High-stakes integration. You're with him through a promotion decision, a difficult firing, a pipeline collapse, a reorg. Real leadership moments, real-time coaching. This is the $7,500 justification.
  • Q4 — Legacy. "What kind of leader do you want to be remembered as?" "How does your team lead when you're not in the room?" He's not working toward eNPS anymore — he's working toward independence. The best outcome: he doesn't need you. And he'll refer everyone who does.

  • Frame the 6-month package correctly: "6 sessions over 6 months — one per month. It's slow by design. You need time between sessions to actually lead differently. We're not going to fix anything in a weekend."

  • Never offer a discount — offer a different package: If they push back on price, don't lower the 6-month. Instead: "We could also start with a 3-session intensive at $2,250 — one session per month over 3 months. You'll know by session 2 if you want to continue." This keeps your rate intact and still closes the deal.

Market context: Executive coaching market rate is $300–800 per session. Your $4,500 for 6 sessions is $750/session — top of market, but justified by your specific track record in sales leadership (not generic coaching). Position it as "sales leadership coaching" not "coaching" — the specificity commands premium.

Chapter 1 Why 179 kr is the exact right price — the psychology of recurring Done ✓ ↓

Pricing a community is completely different from pricing a course or a coaching package. You're not selling a transformation — you're selling access and belonging. The price needs to be high enough to signal value and low enough that people don't cancel when they have a busy month. 179 kr lands perfectly on that edge.

The 179 kr logic
  • Under the 200 kr threshold: There's a real psychological barrier at round numbers. 179 kr feels qualitatively different from 200 kr — it's in a lower "tier" of the brain's purchasing system. It doesn't require justification the way 200+ does.
  • Marcus's frame of reference: He spends 150–200 kr on lunch without thinking. He pays 179 kr/month for Spotify. If the FTR community content is better than his Spotify discovery playlist, it's worth it. And it is — because it's directly applicable to his job.
  • The cancellation threshold: People cancel memberships when the value-to-price ratio tips below a mental threshold. At 179 kr, the bar is low. He won't cancel during a busy month because "it's only 179 kr." At 500 kr, he would. This is by design — you want him to stay, not sprint.
  • Compounding math is the point: 50 members = 8,950 kr/month. 100 members = 17,900 kr/month. 200 members = 35,800 kr/month. This is not your main revenue stream — it's the floor that pays the bills while you run cohorts and 1:1s. At 500 members it's 1,074,000 kr/year. That's a business by itself.
  • Never raise the founding price: Lock in your first 200 members at 179 kr forever — as a founding member benefit. This creates urgency, loyalty, and a group of people who feel they got a deal. They'll be your most vocal advocates.

Chapter 2 What belongs in the paid tier — and what's free Done ✓ ↓

A community without a clear reason to pay is just a group chat. The paid tier needs to have something that Marcus genuinely can't get for free elsewhere — and that he would miss if he cancelled. That "something" doesn't need to be expensive to create. It needs to be specifically useful to him.

Free tier (everyone)
  • Access to the basic community channel — introductions, wins, questions
  • Your weekly LinkedIn content (they already have this — just reframed as a community benefit)
  • Access to the free FTR resources: Aura Profile, public whitepapers, freebies
Paid tier — 179 kr/month (must haves)
  • Monthly live Q&A call: 45 minutes. Open format. Marcus brings a leadership challenge from the past month. You answer it live. 10–20 people on the call — high-quality, low-effort content. Record it for those who can't make it.
  • Templates and tools: Every template you build for the cohort and 1:1 — Energy Log, Session Prep template, Target Setting framework, 1:1 checklist, Core Values workshop, 30-60-90 plan — goes into the paid tier. Real tools Marcus uses in his actual job.
  • Member-only content drops: 1–2x/month. A deeper cut of a LinkedIn post. A framework breakdown. A reading recommendation with your specific take. Things that didn't make the LinkedIn algorithm but that your most engaged followers actually want.
  • Early access and discounts: Course launches, cohort applications, and new products — members get access first and at a discount. 30% off the video course. Priority application to the cohort. This makes membership feel like insider status.
  • The "ask Joa" thread: A dedicated channel where members can ask questions between calls. You respond to 2–3 per week. This is the async version of the monthly Q&A — and it keeps the community active between call dates.

Chapter 3 Seeding the community with FTR's 3 content wheels Done ✓ ↓

You already produce 3 posts per week on LinkedIn. Every one of those posts is also community content. The 3 content wheels — Coaching Moments, Leadership in Practice, and The Personal Foundation — map directly to what the community needs to stay engaged. You're not creating more content. You're routing it to two places instead of one.

The 3 wheels inside the community
  • Coaching Moments (Wheel 1): Real conversations, reframes, Seedster-style observations. On LinkedIn: short, universal. In the community: the full context — what the person was dealing with, the exact question you asked, what shifted. Members get the version that's too specific for the algorithm.
  • Leadership in Practice (Wheel 2): Observations from your work at Lunar, hiring decisions, team dynamics. On LinkedIn: framed as universal principles. In the community: your actual take — including the parts where you're still figuring it out. The unfiltered version builds more trust than the polished one.
  • The Personal Foundation (Wheel 3): Energy, awareness, the whole person, sleep and performance. On LinkedIn: awareness-level content. In the community: the practices — what you actually do, what works, what the Energy Log looks like for you this week. Members want the how, not just the what.
Community seeding rhythm
  • Monday: Weekly intention post — "This week I'm focused on [X]. What's yours?" Starts the week with community energy.
  • Wednesday: A resource or tool drop — template, framework, or a 3-minute voice note with a specific leadership insight. Something they can use that day.
  • Friday: "Win of the week" thread — you share first, then members follow. Builds momentum and creates a record of what's working for people in real time.

Chapter 5 The compounding math — from 50 to 500 members Done ✓ ↓

Membership revenue is slow to start and fast to compound. The first 50 members are the hardest — you're seeding a community culture by yourself. From 50 to 200, the community starts to do the work. From 200 to 500, it runs. This chapter makes the math concrete so you know exactly what you're building toward — and why it's worth the slow start.

The compounding table (179 kr/month)
  • 50 members: 8,950 kr/month · 107,400 kr/year. This covers your fixed costs and gives you a real base to build from. Realistic in 6–9 months from launch.
  • 100 members: 17,900 kr/month · 214,800 kr/year. This is the point where the community stops feeling like a side project and starts feeling like a business line. Realistic in 12–18 months.
  • 200 members: 35,800 kr/month · 429,600 kr/year. Combined with 2 cohort rounds and 4–6 active 1:1 clients, this is a ~1M kr/year business. This is Year 2–3.
  • 500 members: 89,500 kr/month · 1,074,000 kr/year. At this point the community is a standalone business. Not a realistic target for Year 1 — but a real target for Year 4+, and worth keeping in sight.

The key insight about churn: At 179 kr, monthly churn is typically 3–7%. At 100 members, you lose 3–7 people and need to replace them. This means you need a slow, continuous acquisition stream — not occasional launch spikes. The 3 content wheels + LinkedIn posting cadence is that stream. Don't let it stop.
