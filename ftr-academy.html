Chapter 1 Teacher vs. facilitator — the mindset shift that changes everything Done ✓ ↓

You spent years getting good at knowing things. As a sales leader you gave answers, set direction, solved problems. That skill got you here. In the cohort it will work against you. The moment you step into teacher mode — talking more than you listen, filling silence with content, giving advice when someone shares a challenge — the cohort becomes a lecture series. And Marcus has been to enough of those.

The teacher vs. facilitator spectrum
  • Teacher: Has the answer. Shares it. Talks more than listens. Creates dependency. Participants leave with your ideas in their heads — not their own. Completion rate of what they actually do: 5–10%.
  • Facilitator: Has the questions. Creates space. Listens more than talks. Creates agency. Participants leave with their own insight — which they generated. Completion rate of what they actually do: 60–80%.
  • The 80/20 rule of facilitation: Your goal is to speak no more than 20% of the time. Ask a question. Let them answer fully. Ask the next question. The group learns from each other — you hold the container, not the content.
  • Your nervous system leads first: Before you can facilitate anyone else's learning, you have to show up regulated. If you walk into the session anxious, rushed, or in your head — the room will mirror it. Foundation isn't just a module topic; it's your facilitator prerequisite.
  • Silence is your most powerful tool. When you ask a question and nobody answers immediately — wait. Count to 10 in your head. The person who breaks the silence is usually the one who needed to say something. Your job is to not be that person.

The core competency: A great facilitator isn't someone who knows everything — it's someone who creates the conditions for other people to discover things themselves. You already have the knowledge. What you're building here is the restraint to not give it away.

Drill 1 — The ask/tell ratio test (20 min)
  • Find a willing person: A colleague, a friend, or someone from your network. Tell them: "I want to practise something. Ask me about a leadership challenge you're dealing with."
  • Round 1 — Teacher mode (5 min): They share their challenge. You respond as you normally would — give advice, share what worked for you, explain what they should do. Let it run for 5 minutes.
  • Round 2 — Facilitator mode (5 min): Same challenge. This time you are only allowed to ask questions. Zero advice. Zero "what I would do is..." Zero "that reminds me of..." Only: "Tell me more." "What does that cost you?" "What have you already tried?" "What do you think is behind that?" Let it run for 5 minutes.
  • Debrief (10 min): Ask them: "Which conversation felt more useful to you?" Ask yourself: "When did I feel the urge to give advice? What triggered it?" Write the answers down. That urge is what you're training out of yourself.

Chapter 2 Your 6-session curriculum — and the run-of-show for Session 1 Done ✓ ↓

3 months. 6 sessions. 90 minutes each. One pillar of F.E.E.L. per session — with Session 1 as orientation and Session 6 as integration and close. This chapter maps the full arc and then drills down into the exact run-of-show for Session 1. You leave this chapter with a document you can facilitate from.

The 6-session arc
  • Session 1 — Orientation: Who's in the room. The 10 pains. Each participant names their Point A honestly — in front of the group. Establish peer pairs. Agree the contract: this only works if you show up as you actually are — not as you think a manager should be. Homework: complete the Energy Log for 7 days before Session 2.
  • Session 2 — F (Foundation): Lead yourself first. The whole person: sleep, movement, nutrition, emotional regulation. The Energy Compass. "You can't give energy you don't have." Participants share from their Energy Log. Hot seat: one person unpacks a moment where their energy derailed something at work.
  • Session 3 — E (Elevation): Feel The Room. Your nervous system is contagious. Polyvagal Theory: how to read the room before you speak. Body language, HeartMath, state management under pressure. Experiment: before every meeting this week — 60-second reset, then observe the first 2 minutes of energy in the room before you say anything.
  • Session 4 — E (Exploration): Lead With Curiosity. Dream→Current→Problem→Probe. The 7 coaching questions. Practise in pairs — one person coaches, one person is coached, then switch. Debrief: what did it feel like to be asked instead of directed? Experiment: this week, replace one answer with a question in every 1:1 with a rep.
  • Session 5 — L (Liberation): Performative Balance. Culture is what happens when you're not in the room. Psychological safety (Edmondson). Radical Candor. Belonging Cues (Coyle). Recruitment through the F.E.E.L. lens. Hot seat: one person brings a team culture challenge. Experiment: run the Core Values conversation with your team before Session 6.
  • Session 6 — Integration: Each participant presents one thing that changed in their team over 3 months. Real metrics. Real stories. You facilitate — you don't teach. They've become each other's evidence. Close: the path forward — video course, 1:1, or next cohort round. Plant the seed. Don't pitch.
Run-of-show template — Session 1 (90 min)
  • 0–5 min — Open: Welcome. "You're here because something about how you lead isn't working the way you want it to — and you know it." State the format: 6 sessions, 3 months, one commitment per session. No spectators. Everyone is in the room.
  • 5–20 min — Check-in: Go around the room. Each person answers two questions: (1) What's your name and your title? (2) Complete this sentence: "The reason I'm really here is..." Let them talk. Don't interrupt. This is your first listen — and the group's first time hearing each other be honest.
  • 20–40 min — The 10 pains: Walk through the 10 pains from Module 0. For each one — pause. "Show of hands if this sounds familiar." Let them laugh. Let them see each other nod. This is the moment the room becomes a room.
  • 40–55 min — Peer pairs: Pair them up deliberately (not randomly). Give them 10 minutes: "Tell your partner your honest Point A — where you actually are, not where you want to be seen." Debrief two or three pairs in the full group.
  • 55–70 min — The contract: Read it together. "This only works if you show up as you actually are. The moment you perform the right answer, you lose the room." Ask: "Is everyone in?" Get verbal confirmation from each person.
  • 70–85 min — Energy Log intro: Explain the homework: track energy level (1–10) three times a day for 7 days — morning, midday, end of day. Note what drove it up or down. Bring it to Session 2. This is their first experiment in Foundation.
  • 85–90 min — Commitment close: Each person states one thing they'll do before the next session. Specific. One sentence. You go last — model what a good commitment sounds like. Post the commitments in the group chat within 10 minutes of ending.
Drill 2 — The run-of-show build (45 min)
  • Open a blank doc. Build the Session 1 run-of-show in full using the template above.
  • For each section: write the exact words you'll say to open it. Not a script — a first sentence. "We're going to go around the room. I want each of you to finish this sentence..."
  • Write the exact check-in question you'll use. Write the 3 pains you'll open with (the ones you know land hardest). Write the exact commitment question at the close.
  • Then: read it out loud. All 90 minutes of it — in fast-forward. Talk through every section as if you're on Zoom. Time yourself. Adjust wherever you run long.
Action step

Complete Drill 2 before Session 1. The run-of-show is your safety net — not a script. You won't follow it perfectly. But having it means when you lose the thread, you have something to come back to. Build it. Read it out loud. Then put it somewhere you can glance at during the session.

Chapter 3 The hot seat — how to coach someone live in front of a group Done ✓ ↓

The hot seat is the 20 minutes of each session that participants remember for months. One person brings a real challenge. You coach them live. The rest of the group watches — and learns from what they witness, not from anything you say. It's also the part most new facilitators are most afraid of. This chapter removes that fear by making you competent before you do it in public.

How to set up the hot seat
  • Open it the same way every session: "We're going to do a hot seat. One person brings a real challenge — something they're actually dealing with this week, not a hypothetical. I'll coach you live. The group watches. Who wants to go?" Wait. Don't fill the silence. Someone will always volunteer.
  • The setup matters: Before you start coaching, set the frame for the group: "While I'm coaching [name], I want you to pay attention to the questions I ask — not the answers. Notice what shifts in [name] when a question lands. We'll debrief at the end."
  • Your only job: stay in Exploration. Ask. Don't advise. When you feel the urge to give an answer — ask a question instead. "What do you think is behind that?" is always better than your answer. Always.
  • The 7 questions you'll use most: "Tell me more." "What's the impact of that?" "What have you already tried?" "What's stopping you?" "What do you think the real problem is?" "What would you do if you weren't afraid of being wrong?" "What do you actually want — not what you think you should want?"
  • Ending the hot seat: Don't solve it. Close with: "What's the most important thing you're taking from this?" Let them name their own insight. Then ask the group: "What did you notice?" Give them 3 minutes to share. The debrief is often more powerful than the coaching itself.

What the group learns by watching: Every person watching the hot seat is mentally applying the questions to their own situation. "When he asked 'what's stopping you' — I realised the answer for myself." You're coaching one person and teaching nine others simultaneously. That's the hot seat's leverage.

Drill 3 — The 15-minute hot seat practice (repeat 3x)
  • Find a willing person: A colleague, a friend, a former client. Someone dealing with a real work challenge — not a fake one. Tell them: "I'm going to ask you questions for 15 minutes about something real. I will not give you advice. You find your own answer."
  • Set a timer for 15 minutes. Record it. Use your phone. You're going to watch it back.
  • Rules during the drill: No advice. No "what I would do is..." No stories about yourself. Only questions. If you catch yourself giving advice — stop mid-sentence and ask a question instead.
  • Watch it back and score yourself: Count the number of times you asked a question vs. made a statement. Ratio target: 80% questions, 20% statements. Note: which question created the most visible shift in the person? Write that question down. That's your best question — use it every session.
  • Do this 3 times before Cohort 1. Each time, your question:statement ratio should improve. By the third session it should feel uncomfortable to give advice — that's when you're ready.
Action step

Book Drill 3 into your calendar right now — three separate 30-minute slots, with three different people. Don't wait until the week before Cohort 1. The hot seat is a muscle. Build it early. Each session: record, watch, score. Your question:statement ratio is your progress metric.

Chapter 4 When it goes wrong — 5 scenarios with exact scripts Done ✓ ↓

Every cohort will have at least one moment that goes sideways. Dead silence. A person who dominates. Someone who challenges you publicly. A participant who breaks down. A session where the energy completely flatlines. Inexperienced facilitators freeze. Experienced ones have a move for each scenario. This chapter gives you those moves — in advance.

Scenario 1 — Dead silence: nobody answers the check-in
  • What happens: You ask the check-in question. Nobody speaks. 10 seconds pass. Then 15. You feel the panic rising.
  • What NOT to do: Rush to fill the silence. Answer it yourself first. Apologise for the question. Change the question.
  • The move: Wait. Count to 15. Then: "I'm going to sit with this silence for a moment — because I think it's telling us something." Pause again. Then: "[Name] — what's on your mind right now?" Direct, warm, non-threatening. Address someone you trust to open. Once one person speaks, the group follows.
Scenario 2 — The dominator: one person takes over
  • What happens: One participant answers every question. Speaks over others. Has an opinion on everything. The group starts to disengage.
  • What NOT to do: Ignore it. Or shut them down in a way that embarrasses them in front of the group.
  • The move: After they speak: "Thank you — that's a strong perspective. I want to hear from someone who sees this differently." Name someone. "Martin — what's your take?" Or, at the start of the next section: "I'm going to ask everyone to hold space this round — I want to hear from the people who haven't spoken yet." Direct, but without singling anyone out negatively.
Scenario 3 — The challenger: someone questions your credibility
  • What happens: "With respect — I've been in sales management for 15 years and this isn't how it works in practice." Or: "Isn't this just common sense dressed up as a framework?"
  • What NOT to do: Get defensive. Over-explain. Compete with their experience.
  • The move: "That's a fair challenge — and I'd rather hear it than not. Tell me more about what you've seen that's different." Lean into it. They usually want to be heard, not to dismantle you. If they push further: "I'm not here to tell you this is the only way. I'm here to offer a frame — you take what's useful and leave what isn't." Then redirect to the group: "Who else has a different experience?"
Scenario 4 — Emotional breakdown: a participant gets visibly emotional
  • What happens: During the hot seat or a vulnerable sharing moment, someone gets teary, their voice breaks, or they go quiet and shut down.
  • What NOT to do: Rush to comfort them. Make it awkward by drawing attention to it. Move on immediately as if it didn't happen.
  • The move: Slow down. Soften your voice. "Take a moment." Pause. Let the silence hold without awkwardness. Then: "What's coming up for you right now?" Let them choose how much to share. Don't interpret or explain what they're feeling. If they need a minute, give it. If they want to continue — follow their lead. The group will trust you more for how you handle this than for anything you teach.
Scenario 5 — Flat energy: the session dies mid-way
  • What happens: 45 minutes in, the energy drops. People are giving short answers. Nobody's leaning in. You're losing the room.
  • What NOT to do: Push through with the planned content. Speak louder or faster. Pretend it isn't happening.
  • The move: Name it. "I'm noticing the energy has shifted — let's pause. What's going on?" Or: disrupt the physical state — "Stand up for 60 seconds." Even on Zoom, asking people to stand, stretch, or turn their camera away and back creates a reset. Then: don't go back to where you were. Ask: "What would make the rest of this session most useful for you?" Let them redirect it.

Drill 4 — The scenario rehearsal (30 min solo)
  • Read through each of the 5 scenarios above. Then close the page.
  • Say each scenario out loud to yourself — as if you're in the session: describe what just happened in one sentence. Then say your response out loud, from memory. Don't look at the script.
  • The goal isn't to memorise it word for word. It's to feel how the response sounds coming out of your mouth — before the adrenaline of a live session makes it hard to access.
  • Write one scenario you personally dread the most. Practise that one three more times.
Action step

Do Drill 4 today — out loud, alone. Then identify your highest-anxiety scenario and practise it with someone you trust. Ask them to play the role. Run through it twice. The moment a scenario stops feeling threatening is the moment you stop being afraid of it happening in Cohort 1.

Chapter 5 Between sessions, filling the room — infrastructure and launch Done ✓ ↓

90% of the learning happens between sessions — when Marcus tries something on his team, it fails, and he has to figure out why. And before any of that can happen, you need 10 Marcuses in the room. This chapter covers both: the between-session infrastructure that keeps the cohort alive, and the 6-week launch sequence that fills it.

Between-session infrastructure
  • Peer pairs from Day 1: Pair participants at Session 1. They check in once a week — 15 minutes each. "What did you try? What happened?" It costs you nothing and increases retention dramatically. You match the pairs deliberately — pick people with complementary blind spots.
  • Experiments, not homework: Every session ends with one experiment. Not a reading — a doing. "Before we meet again: use the Dream→Current→Problem flow in one real conversation with a rep. Come ready to debrief what happened." Doing assignments have 3x the completion rate of reading assignments.
  • "Win of the week" thread: Every Monday in the group chat, you post first: "What's one win from last week — big or small?" They follow. This keeps the cohort alive between sessions and creates a record of progress that participants can look back on in Session 6.
  • Energy check-ins mid-week: On Wednesday — a voice note to the group. 60 seconds. "Halfway through the week — how's the experiment going? DM me if you're stuck." This takes 2 minutes of your time and makes participants feel seen between sessions.
The 6-week launch sequence for Cohort 1
  • 6 weeks before start: Public LinkedIn post. "I'm building something for sales managers who want to stop managing and start leading. Not a course — a 3-month program with 10 people. Following for details." No price. No details. Collect interest in comments and DMs.
  • 4 weeks before: Open a simple waitlist. One-question form: "Tell me the biggest challenge you face as a sales manager right now." Applications, not sign-ups. Every person who applies gets a discovery call — use the F.E.E.L. sales flow. Dream→Current→Problem→Close. "Fuck yes or fuck no." Don't chase maybes.
  • 2 weeks before: Close enrollment. Email confirmed participants: the curriculum doc, the Zoom link, the Energy Log template, and a voice note from you telling them what to expect in Session 1. Make them feel like they've already started.
  • After Session 1: Ask for feedback immediately. One question: "What was most valuable, and what would you change?" Use this to shape Session 2. Show them their feedback changed something. That's community starting to form.
  • The pricing frame: $3,500 for 3 months = $1,166/month. Marcus's salary is probably 60–80,000 DKK/month. He's spending 8,500 kr to get better at managing a team generating millions. If the ROI conversation feels hard — you haven't asked enough questions about what it costs him to stay where he is.

The 1:1 Advisory is your deepest product and your most demanding one. You're not a therapist and you're not a consultant — you're a thinking partner who creates a structured arc of change over 6 or 12 months. $4,500 for 6 sessions. $7,500 for 12 sessions. Chapter 1 First call — mapping Point A with the F.E.E.L. flow Done ✓ ↓

The first session is the most important one. It sets the emotional contract, surfaces the real problem (which is rarely the one they say out loud on the discovery call), and establishes you as someone who listens before they speak. Done well, the client leaves the first session already thinking differently. Done poorly, they wonder if this was worth $4,500.

Session 1 structure (60 min)
  • 0–10 min — Why are they here? "Tell me what made you say yes to this." Let them talk. Don't interrupt. You're listening for the real Point A — the one underneath the one they're saying. A Captain will say "I want to be a better leader." What he means is "my team doesn't trust me and I don't know why."
  • 10–30 min — Point A (Current State): What's working. What isn't. What they're not willing to say yet. Use the Explore questions: "Tell me more." Never "why." "What's the impact of that on your team?" "What does it cost you when this happens?" Let them feel the gap — don't create it for them.
  • 30–50 min — Point B (Dream): "What does success look like 6 months from now — specifically?" Not feelings. Evidence. "My team's eNPS goes from 40 to 70." "I stop having the same performance conversation with the same person every month." "I walk into Monday without dread." Specific Point B = the spine of the whole engagement.
  • 50–58 min — Contract: Agree on 2–3 focus themes. Not 10 — 2 or 3. These become the recurring lens for every session. Write them down together. "We're going to spend the next 6 months on: coaching instead of directing, energy management under pressure, and the one team member you're avoiding."
  • 58–60 min — Logistics: Communication between sessions (voice note? Email? WhatsApp?), what to prepare before each call, rescheduling policy. Keep it simple. Make it feel effortless to be your client.

Chapter 2 The recurring session — 60 minutes with the F.E.E.L. arc Done ✓ ↓

Sessions 2 through 6 (or 12) follow the same rhythm. Consistency is trust. Your client knows what to expect — and can prepare accordingly. The structure is simple: show up, check in, close the loop from last time, do the work, synthesise, commit. Repeat until Point B is real.

Recurring session structure (60 min)
  • 0–5 min — Check-in: "What's your energy today? What's on your mind that needs to get out of the way before we start?" A Captain will say "fine" and want to get to business. Push gently: "And what's the one thing you haven't told anyone yet this week?" That's where the real session starts.
  • 5–15 min — Accountability: "What did you commit to last time?" "What actually happened?" No judgment. Pure curiosity. "What did you learn from that?" If they didn't do it — explore why. That gap is usually the coaching.
  • 15–45 min — Core work: One challenge, one pattern, one insight. Ask more than you tell. Use the 7 coaching questions. When you feel the urge to give advice — ask instead. "What do you think is stopping you?" is always more powerful than your answer.
  • 45–55 min — Synthesis: "What was the most important insight from today?" Let them say it — not you. They name it, they own it. If they can't name it, you haven't done enough Explore work.
  • 55–60 min — Commitment: One specific action before the next session. What. When. How they'll know they did it. This is the "Lead" step — they close their own session.

The ask vs. tell ratio: Track how often you speak vs. how often you ask. If you're speaking more than 30% of the time, you're consulting, not coaching. The goal: ask a question, let them answer fully, then ask the next question. Your value is in the questions — not the answers.

Chapter 3 The 6-month vs. 12-month arc — what changes each quarter Done ✓ ↓

One session per month is enough. Marcus will push back — "isn't that too infrequent?" The answer: no. Because between sessions he's doing the work. He's leading his team. He's running the experiments. He's failing and learning. Your 60 minutes is the container that makes sense of all of that. Here's what the arc looks like across 6 and 12 months.

6-month arc ($4,500)
  • Month 1 — Discovery: Deep listening. Establish trust. Map Point A fully. Don't rush to solutions. The patterns that need changing rarely show up in the first session — they show up in month 2.
  • Months 2–3 — Pattern identification: Spot the 1–2 behaviours that are holding him back. Name them together. "I notice that every time your team pushes back, you give them the answer instead of letting them find it. What do you think is behind that?"
  • Months 4–5 — Behavioural experiments: He tries new things. You debrief what happened. This is where the F.E.E.L. methodology moves from theory to muscle memory.
  • Month 6 — Consolidation: What changed? What's the evidence? What does Marcus want to keep working on? Plant the seed for a 12-month extension or cohort participation. Don't make it a hard close — make it a natural conversation.
12-month arc ($7,500)
  • Q1 — Same as 6-month months 1–2. Discovery, trust, first patterns emerging.
  • Q2 — Identity work. Marcus has been running on his sælger-identity for years. In Q2, you go deeper: "Who are you as a leader — separate from your results?" This is where the PI profile and Social Needs lens become tools. A Captain needs Power — how does he meet that need through his team instead of over them?
  • Q3 — High-stakes integration. You're with him through a promotion decision, a difficult firing, a pipeline collapse, a reorg. Real leadership moments, real-time coaching. This is the $7,500 justification.
  • Q4 — Legacy. "What kind of leader do you want to be remembered as?" "How does your team lead when you're not in the room?" He's not working toward eNPS anymore — he's working toward independence. The best outcome: he doesn't need you. And he'll refer everyone who does.
Action step

Build a one-page "Engagement Map" for each package. List what you'll focus on each month. Share it at the start of the engagement. It makes the client feel like they're buying a plan — not just access to you. And it gives you something to come back to when sessions drift from the focus themes you agreed on in Session 1.

Chapter 4 Holding the frame at $4,500 — how to price yourself without apologising Done ✓ ↓

You've had 100,000+ sales conversations and generated €45M in career sales. You built a team with 80+ eNPS. That track record is not a credential — it's the product. Marcus is not paying for your time. He's paying for the transformation that only happens because you've been where he's trying to go. Here's how you talk about price without flinching.

The pricing conversation
  • Lead with ROI, not value: "The 6-month program is $4,500. Your team is probably generating $2–5M in revenue. If our work together improves your team's performance by 10%, that's $200–500K in additional revenue. The program pays for itself in the first month."
  • Use Power of No on price: "If budget is the issue, we shouldn't do this." Not a threat — a genuine disqualifier. When Marcus hears "you don't have to" his Captain instinct kicks in: "I decide what I spend money on." The resistance drops.
  • Frame the 6-month package correctly: "6 sessions over 6 months — one per month. It's slow by design. You need time between sessions to actually lead differently. We're not going to fix anything in a weekend."
  • The 12-month frame: "The 12-month engagement is for leaders who are serious about fundamentally changing how they lead — not just getting better at a few things. It's $7,500 because it includes the hard work: the identity shifts, the high-stakes moments, the stuff that doesn't happen in 6 months."
  • Never offer a discount — offer a different package: If they push back on price, don't lower the 6-month. Instead: "We could also start with a 3-session intensive at $2,250 — one session per month over 3 months. You'll know by session 2 if you want to continue." This keeps your rate intact and still closes the deal.

Market context: Executive coaching market rate is $300–800 per session. Your $4,500 for 6 sessions is $750/session — top of market, but justified by your specific track record in sales leadership (not generic coaching). Position it as "sales leadership coaching" not "coaching" — the specificity commands premium.

Action step

Practice the pricing conversation out loud three times. Say the number — "$4,500" — without trailing off, without adding "but" after it, without apologising. Then say the ROI framing. Then stop talking. Silence after the price is where most coaches lose the frame. Practice holding it.

Chapter 5 Turning a 1:1 client into an advocate — the referral flywheel Done ✓ ↓

The best marketing for FTR is a Marcus who walks back into his company changed. His VP of Sales notices. His team notices. His peers notice. That Marcus doesn't just refer clients — he refers them with conviction. Here's how to turn the engagement itself into a referral engine.

The advocate conversion
  • Month 3 check-in: Ask Marcus: "Who else in your company has this problem?" Not a referral ask — a coaching question. He's spent 3 months thinking about leadership. He now sees these patterns everywhere. He'll name 3 people without you having to sell anything.
  • Make results visible: Help Marcus articulate what changed. Not feelings — evidence. "My team's pipeline velocity increased 23%. My Monday 1:1s take 20 minutes instead of 45. I had a difficult conversation I'd been avoiding for 8 months." These become your testimonials — with his permission.
  • The cohort invite: In Session 5 or 6: "I'm running a cohort in [month] with 10 sales managers working on exactly what we've been working on.
