Signature Series Ethan Shaw method

Two recurring formats that run every week. The Room Breakdown (Fri) + The Monday Question (Mon). Same structure every time — so the algorithm and your audience both know what to expect. Drag in status badges as you work through them.

Series 1 The Room Breakdown Every Friday

Format: Setting → What most people do → What I did → What it changed. Always a real story from a real room. Builds authority through experience, not opinion.

TRB #1 L The pause that closed the deal
Idea Show ↓
A prospect pushed back hard in the middle of my pitch. Not a question. A challenge. "Your competitors charge half this and deliver the same results." I felt it. The flush. The urge to defend. I didn't. I sat in it for a moment. Then I said: "That might be true. What would make it worth more to you?" He paused. Then he walked me through exactly what he needed. That conversation became the proposal we closed. The pause was the skill. Not the script that came after it. Emotional regulation isn't a soft skill. It's the one thing between you and a lost deal.
TRB #2 L I said no to a client with budget
Idea Show ↓
They had the budget. They wanted to start immediately. I said no. Something was off from the first call. Every time I asked about the team, they redirected. "We just need the training. They'll come around." Teams don't come around. They need a real conversation first — and someone willing to have it. I told them I wasn't the right fit for where they were. The call ended quietly. Three weeks later, they came back. They'd had the conversation. Now they were ready. We worked together for four months. The hardest skill in sales isn't closing. It's knowing when not to.
TRB #3 C My worst sales stretch — and what it was actually about
Idea Show ↓
I was in my worst sales stretch in years. Cold pipeline. Missed calls. Deals going quiet. I blamed the market. The product. The timing. Then a coach asked me: "What else is going on right now?" I talked for 20 minutes about things that had nothing to do with sales. She didn't say anything for a moment. Then: "Do you notice how similar that sounds to what you just described?" She was right. I was avoiding hard conversations at home. So I was avoiding them in the pipeline too. When I fixed one, the other fixed itself. Sales doesn't lie. It shows you exactly who you are under pressure.
TRB #4 L Month one: zero deals
Idea Show ↓
My first month in insurance sales, I made zero. Second month, one deal. Third month, two. Every morning I showed up. Made the calls. Followed up. Did it again. No big moments. No glory. No one watching. My manager pulled me aside at month four: "Most people quit by now." I asked him why they quit. "Because they think it's not working. It's always working. They just can't see it yet." I thought about that sentence on every bad week after. The most durable competitive advantage in sales isn't skill. It's still being here in three years.
TRB #5 P Why not me?
Idea Show ↓
For years I was looking for a coach who taught leadership the way I'd experienced it. Not just techniques. The whole thing. The energy. The culture. The person behind the role. I couldn't find them. So one day I asked myself: why not me? I wrote down ten reasons it wasn't possible. Then I sat with each one. None of them held up. Someone with less experience had already started. Someone with more fear had already taken the leap. Someone with half the skills had already figured it out. That wasn't comfort. That was a challenge. The question doesn't give you permission. It removes the excuse you've been hiding behind.
Series 2 The Monday Question Every Monday

Format: One question. 2–3 lines of context. No answer given. Short. Makes people stop and think. Drives comments because the question IS the content.

TMQ #1 P What state do you want to walk in with?
Idea Show ↓
The Monday Question: When did you last feel triggered in a conversation — and choose your response instead of letting your gut choose it? That pause is a skill. And like every skill, it gets stronger when you practice it. Where did you feel it this week? 👇
TMQ #2 L Who haven't you really listened to?
Idea Show ↓
The Monday Question: What's a pattern showing up in your results right now? Now ask: where else is that pattern showing up in your life? Sales doesn't lie. It shows you exactly who you are under pressure. What do you see? 👇
TMQ #3 P When did you last do something for the first time?
Idea Show ↓
The Monday Question: What's the thing you keep saying you'll do "when you're ready"? Write down why not now. Then look at those reasons honestly. Someone with less experience already started. Someone with more fear already took the leap. Why not you? 👇

#1 C 4Power — Two engineers who never asked the end user
Show ↓
They had built a great product. Machine learning. Energy optimisation for holiday homes. But they didn't know who was supposed to pay for it. Was it the owners? The energy companies? The municipalities? I asked: Have you asked them? Silence. They were three steps removed from the end user. And they had never spoken to one. That's what you do. You build. You believe. You confirm each other that it's a good idea. And then you're surprised when nobody buys. Ten conversations with end users will tell you more than six months of development. Build on knowledge. Not on belief.
#2 C Mick — He had 7,000 followers and zero B2B clients
Show ↓
He had 7,000 followers on YouTube. And zero B2B clients. Mick teaches Ukrainians Danish. Strong platform. Real credibility. But the money wasn't coming. He asked: "How do I find B2B clients?" I asked: "What would you do if you were your own client?" He paused for a moment. "I would... search on YouTube. Find the person who teaches Danish. Contact them." Yes. You're not just the teacher. You're the product. You're the proof. The people who should pay for your services see exactly the same thing as your 7,000 followers. It's not a lead problem. It's a visibility problem. The question isn't: "How do I find clients?" The question is: "Can they find me?"
#3 C Kasper+Jesper — The worst thing in sales is calling without a reason
Show ↓
The worst thing I know as a salesperson is calling without a reason. Kasper and Jesper have 40 years of combined experience. They cover half of Denmark's electricity meters. The question was: How do you scale sales when the solution is complex and the clients are conservative? I asked: "What always happens with your clients?" Regulatory updates. Renewal cycles. Reports from the Authority. That's your sales calendar. Not marketing leads. Not cold calls. Find the events that always happen. Use them as a reason to call. "Hi Lars. I know you're due for renewal soon. Have you thought about it yet?" That's not sales. That's service. And they'll always take that call. Most salespeople call without a reason. The best ones call at the right moment.
#5 C Bia — Your experience only lives if people know it
Show ↓
Bia produces curtains in Denmark. 141-year-old company. 24 employees. World-class craftsmanship. She asked: "Can we find a young person to handle our videos?" I said: No. A 25-year-old can say the exact words. But they can't tell the story the same way. You have years in this industry. You know what it costs to do it right. You know what happens when you don't. That's not knowledge they can learn in two months. It's knowledge they haven't lived yet. Your experience is your brand. But only if people know about it. You can't scale production. But you can scale your knowledge. And that's what attracts the clients you actually want.
#6 C Christina — "I'm not a sales type"
Show ↓
She was a nurse for 10 years. Now she was supposed to sell. The first thing she said was: "I'm not a sales type." I asked: What's a sales type? She described someone who pushes. Persuades. Doesn't listen. "That's not sales," I said. "That's just pressure with a target." Sales is helping someone make the right decision. For 10 years as a nurse she asked questions. Found the problem. Suggested the solution. The patient decided. That's exactly what happens in a good sales conversation. She just didn't realise she already knew how to do it. Sales is not a personality type. It's an approach. And you might already have it.
#7 C CEO day 1 — "What do I do in the first 90 days?"
Show ↓
It was his first day as CEO. 200 clients. 30 employees. High expectations. He asked: "What do I do in the first 90 days?" I said: Nothing. "...Nothing?" Act like a sponge. Absorb everything. What do clients want? What drives the team? What actually works? Most new leaders start by proving themselves. The best ones start by understanding. Authority isn't built by talking. It's built by knowing. And you don't know that on day one. There's a difference between leading and having a title. The title you got on day one. Trust is something you have to earn. It takes time. And it starts with listening.
#8 C Adtool — They wanted three new markets. I said: Sell nothing.
Show ↓
They wanted to enter three new markets. Good solution. Strong Danish track record. But they didn't know: what works in Sweden? In Norway? In Northern Germany? Most people start by pitching. We talked about asking instead. Market research isn't just research. It's your best sales meeting. Call a potential client and say: "We're considering entering your market. What's your biggest challenge right now?" Two things happen. 1. You get knowledge no competitor has. 2. They remember you. When you call again, you're not a salesperson. You're the one who listened. That's a different conversation. Most people use market research to validate a decision. The best use it to start a relationship.
#9 C Tradespeople to Australia — "Am I interrupting?" is the worst opening
Show ↓
They help young tradespeople get to Australia, New Zealand and Canada. 700 leads a month. The only company in Europe doing it. The first thing I said was: Stop asking "am I interrupting?" It sounds polite. But it's the world's easiest escape route. "Actually, now isn't great" — and the conversation is over. Replace it with: "Hope I caught you at a good time." Now it's hard to say no. The same principle applies to "why?" "Why did you take that test?" sounds like an innocent question. But many of us got told off with that word as children. We automatically go on the defensive. Say instead: "Would you tell me a bit more about that?" Exactly the same question. Half the resistance. And most importantly: The person who clicked on a salary test to see what they'd earn in Australia — they've been thinking about this for months. It's not an impulse. It's a dream they've been carrying around. Ask into it. Treat it with respect. And they'll sell to themselves.
#4 C Tobias — "I always hit a wall when I present the solution"
Show ↓
Tobias runs a marketing agency for tradespeople. He said: "I always hit a wall when I present the solution." I said: That's because you're presenting the solution too early. There are four phases in a good sales meeting. 1. Dream 2. Problem 3. Feature 4. Solution Most people jump straight to solution. But that only works if the other person has already told you exactly what they dream about and what's holding them back. Phase one: Dream. "Where do you want to be in 12 months?" "What would that mean for you?" "Why is that important to you?" Let them paint the picture themselves. All the way. Phase two: Problem. "What's the reason you're not there yet?" "What's the consequence for you if this continues?" Now they've put words to the pain themselves. Phase three: Feature. Which specific things do they want solved? Let them tell you. Phase four: Solution. Now your solution fits exactly what they've described themselves. That's not manipulation. That's proper listening. Tobias doesn't hit a wall with the solution. He hits a wall because he skipped the first three phases.

L Team Core Values — Who are we at our best?
Show ↓
For two weeks my team worked on one question: Who are we when we're at our best? Not who we want to be. Who we already ARE when things are working. Because values don't work if they're something leadership decided. They work when the whole team knows them. And recognises themselves in them. We landed on four: Authenticity. Shared ownership. Energetic positivity. Performance needs fun. Not because they sounded good. But because everyone in the room said: yes, that's us. That's what makes the difference. Not the words on the wall. Ask your team who they are at their best. The answers will surprise you.
C Strategic questioning — The one who asks the questions is in control
Show ↓
The one who asks the questions is in control. That sounds like a sales quote. But it's more fundamental than that. Questions direct thoughts. Thoughts direct decisions. If I ask: "What's your biggest challenge?" You think about challenges. If I ask: "What would it mean for you if this was solved?" You think about possibilities. Exactly the same meeting. Two completely different places you end up. Most salespeople ask questions to find reasons to pitch. The best ask questions to understand. Next time you go into a meeting: write down three questions you want to ask. Not to get answers. But to guide the conversation somewhere both parties gain something.
L Performance needs fun — We don't choose between performance and fun
Show ↓
We don't choose between high performance and fun. We insist on both. That's one of my team's values. And it sounds easy. But it's actually a deliberate choice you have to make every day. Because it's easy to let performance overshadow everything else. Results. Targets. Numbers. Forecasts. And suddenly the only thing we talk about is pipeline. We have a rule: Celebrate the wins before moving on to the next. Gamify the boring things. Protect the energy, because it drives performance. A salesperson in good energy closes more. That's not a feeling. That's a number. Performance needs fun isn't a fluffy concept. It's a business strategy.
C The close technique — Let them sell to themselves
Show ↓
Most salespeople are nervous about asking for the deal. I've never understood it. If you've done your job properly, the close isn't persuasion. It's confirmation. I often use this: "Based on what we've talked about today — do you feel this roughly lives up to the picture you had?" Yes, no or maybe. If they say yes: "Great. Why?" Now they start selling to themselves. Listen. Don't interrupt. If they say no: what's missing? Now you have something to work with. If they say maybe: that's the only answer you can't accept. Many closers are nervous about the close because they skipped everything important. Dream → Problem → Feature → Solution. Prepare well, and the close takes care of itself.
P The whole person — I don't hire a title
Show ↓
I don't hire a title. I hire a person. That sounds like a cliché. But many people do recruitment backwards. Find a candidate with the right skills. Hope the rest fits. What determines whether a salesperson succeeds is rarely skills. It's energy. Self-awareness. The ability to keep going. What happens when they lose a deal. What drives them when no one is watching. I always spend time understanding the person behind the position. What are you engaged with when you're not working? What are you most proud of? What's the hardest thing you've been through? The answer tells me more than the CV. Sales is a mirror of your life. Hire slowly. And hire the whole person.
C Think outside in — People buy themselves free of problems
Show ↓
We tend to start with ourselves. Our product. Our solution. Our approach. And then we wonder why the client doesn't follow. Try it the other way around. Start with them. What are they focused on? What keeps them up at night? What's the consequence if this problem doesn't get solved? And ask yourself: What would I do if I were them? That's the exercise that changes the most sales conversations. Not because you change your product. But because you change the angle you present it from. People don't buy solutions. They buy themselves free of problems. Find the problem. Use the language around it. Then the solution takes care of itself.
L Celebrate the small things — Performance is a habit
Show ↓
One of my salespeople booked their first meeting with a CFO. That's not a closed deal. That's not revenue. But it deserved a reaction. Not because it's a huge achievement. But because energy is fuel. And fuel needs to be topped up along the way — not just at the finish line. Many teams celebrate the quarterly result. The best teams celebrate the Monday call that ends in a meeting. Celebrate the small things. While they happen. Because the salesperson who gets recognised for the first CFO meeting makes three more calls. That's not management fluff. That's simple human psychology. Performance is a habit. And habits are built with immediate feedback.
C You already have the answer — Most people already know
Show ↓
I rarely give advice. That sounds strange, because people come to me for advice. But nine times out of ten they already have the answer. They know what they need to do. They just need someone to ask the right question. "What would you recommend someone else do in your situation?" Pause. And then they start saying what they knew all along. It's not because I'm lazy. It's because it makes a difference whether you find the answer yourself — or whether someone hands it to you. The first one sticks. The second one you forget on the way out of the room. The best conversations are the ones where the other person says: "I knew that. I just didn't realise I knew it."
P Energy as a compass — The most underrated competitive advantage
Show ↓
I train 4–5 times a week. Not because I'm obsessed with my body. But because that's where I make my best decisions. When I'm in good physical shape, I'm in better mental shape. Clearer. Calmer. More patient. That applies to sales. It applies to leadership. It applies to everything. We talk a lot about mindset. But mindset lives in the body. And yet many of us treat the body as something secondary. Something that can wait until the weekend, the holiday or "when things calm down." Things don't calm down. Energy isn't something you have. It's something you build. Daily. It's the most underrated competitive advantage I know.
Overview C Seedster Marbella — 9 conversations. 9 companies. 4 hours.
Show ↓
Last week I sat in Marbella and coached 9 founders over four hours. One after the other. Seedster and Lunar had gathered a group of people building something real. And I got to sit across from all of them. Here's what I took home: Most people already know what they need to do. They just need someone to ask them the right question. Sales isn't about persuasion. It's about understanding — and helping the other person see what they can't see themselves. A tech founder was building on assumptions, not data. A solo operator was afraid to call because she felt like she was interrupting. A marketing consultant hit a wall every time he presented the solution too early. Same problem. Different shape. The best sales conversations are the ones where the client sells to themselves. Your job is just to set the stage. Thank you to Seedster and Lunar for inviting me. And thank you to all nine for showing up with open minds. Four hours I won't forget. #coaching #sales #founder #seedster

C Say No to a Client
Show ↓
I said no to a client last year. They had budget. They wanted the engagement. But something was off. They wanted me to fix their team while avoiding the conversation their team actually needed to have. That never works. The best outcomes I've had — they happened because the client was ready to hear hard things. When someone isn't, you can do everything right and it still won't land. The hardest skill in sales isn't closing. It's knowing when to walk away. Not every yes is worth taking.
P I have 50 goals written down
Show ↓
I have 50 goals written down. Not a vision board. Not quarterly OKRs. 50 specific things I want to do, build, feel, and become. Some are professional. Some are deeply personal. Some are embarrassingly small. The point isn't to achieve all 50. The point is clarity. When you know what you want, you stop saying yes to everything that doesn't move you toward it. Most people don't know what they want with enough precision. They have a vague direction. Write 50 things. Specific things. It's harder than it sounds. That's why it works.
P Why. Why Not. Why Not Me.
Show ↓
Three questions I ask myself when I'm hesitating. 1. Why do I want this? 2. Why haven't I done it yet? 3. Why not me? The first gets you honest about motivation. The second exposes the real obstacle. The third breaks the ceiling. Most people stop at "why not me?" because they can list ten reasons. But those reasons don't hold up under scrutiny. Someone with less experience started. Someone with more fear took the leap. Someone with half the skills figured it out. Why not you? The question doesn't give you permission. It removes the excuse you've been hiding behind.
P Sales Is a Mirror of Your Life
Show ↓
The best salespeople I know have one thing in common. They do the work on themselves. Not because they read self-help books. Because they understand: how you do anything is how you do everything. If you avoid hard conversations at home, you avoid them in sales. If you can't hold discipline in your personal life, you can't hold it in pipeline management. If you're scared to be rejected by people you love, you're scared to be rejected by a prospect. Sales doesn't lie. It shows you exactly who you are under pressure. That's why self-development isn't separate from sales training. It is the sales training.
P The most underestimated sales skill? Emotional regulation.
Show ↓
The most underestimated sales skill? Emotional regulation. Not charisma. Not closing techniques. Not objection handling. The ability to stay calm when a prospect pushes back. The ability to sit in silence without filling it. The ability to receive a no without collapsing. Most salespeople are reactive. A signal goes in, a reaction comes out. The best ones have a pause. That pause is everything. It's where you choose your response instead of your gut choosing it for you. You can train this. Start by noticing when you're triggered. Name it. Then choose what to do next. The pause gets longer. The results get better.
C Sales = Discipline + The Ability to Keep Going
Show ↓
Talent gets you in the door. Discipline keeps you in the game. I've seen talented salespeople flame out because they couldn't handle consistency. No big moments. No glory calls. Just: make the calls, follow up, show up, repeat. And I've seen average people outlast everyone else simply by refusing to stop. The most durable competitive advantage in sales isn't skill. It's the decision to still be here in three years. Most people quit before they get good. They quit when it's hard. When the pipeline is cold. When the last three deals fell through. That's exactly when you can't quit. Stay in the game long enough, and the game starts to play differently.
P Self-Development Is Your Best Contribution
Show ↓
I used to think self-development was selfish. Time spent on myself felt like time stolen from work, from family, from everything else. Then I realized: the best thing I can bring to any room is a better version of me. When I'm clearer, I ask better questions. When I'm more regulated, I hold better conversations. When I know myself, I help others know themselves. Self-development isn't separate from leadership. It is leadership. The leader who never works on themselves eventually runs out of things to give. Invest in yourself. Not because it feels good. Because everyone around you gets a better version of you when you do.
P Don't Put a Mask On
Show ↓
I tried to be more polished once. More professional. Safer answers. Careful wording. It didn't work. Not because people didn't respond to it. But because I couldn't sustain it. The version of you that tries to be what people want is exhausting to maintain. And people feel it anyway. The most magnetic people I know are magnetic because they don't pretend. They say what they think. They admit what they don't know. They show up the same way every time. Authenticity isn't just more honest. It's more effective. Because the person you're trying to impress doesn't trust performance. They trust people.
C The Importance of Qualification
Show ↓
Most bad sales experiences come from one mistake. Skipping qualification. You take the call. You run the demo. You spend two hours building a proposal. And then they say: "We don't really have budget for this." You should have known that in the first five minutes. Qualification isn't gatekeeping. It's respect. Respect for your time. Respect for theirs. Four things to confirm before going further: Is there a real problem? Do they have the authority to act? Do they have budget? Is the timing right? If you can't say yes to all four, you're not in a sales conversation. You're in a free consulting session. Qualify early. Qualify often. Your conversion rate will thank you.
C Frame Setting = Control
Show ↓
Whoever sets the frame controls the conversation. Most salespeople walk into a meeting and accept the frame the prospect has already set. "We're just exploring options." "We've been happy with our current provider." "Talk me through your pricing." Those are their frames. And if you accept them, you're playing their game. Set your own frame early. "Before we dive in — I'd like to understand what success actually looks like for you in the next 90 days." Now you're the one driving. Frame setting isn't manipulation. It's structure. It helps both of you get somewhere useful faster. If you don't set the frame, someone else will.
C "Maybe" is the most expensive word in sales
Show ↓
"Maybe" is the most expensive word in sales. It sounds like progress. It feels like hope. But it's usually just delayed rejection with a polite face on. I don't accept maybe. Not because I'm pushy. Because it wastes time — mine and theirs. If someone can't tell me yes or no, I ask directly: "What would need to be true for this to be a yes?" Now we're having a real conversation. Either they tell me what's missing and we can work with it. Or they tell me it's not right and we can both move on. The best sales cultures I've seen don't celebrate pipeline. They celebrate clarity. Fuck yes or fuck no. Everything else is just noise.
C The Power of Silence
Show ↓
I asked a question. Then I stopped talking. For seven full seconds. It felt like forever. The prospect shifted. Started talking. And told me exactly what I needed to know to close the deal. Most salespeople are terrified of silence. They fill it. With features. With anecdotes. With reassurance. But silence isn't a problem. Silence is an invitation. It says: I'm waiting. I believe what you have to say matters. The next time you ask a question, let the silence do its work. Seven seconds. Don't flinch. The answer that comes after the pause is usually the real one.
C Make the Signature a Non-Event
Show ↓
The worst thing you can do at the close is make it feel like a big deal. Because big deals make people nervous. Nervous people slow down. Ask more questions. Want to "think it over." The goal of a well-run sales process is that by the time you reach the signature, it feels inevitable. Not pressured. Not rushed. Just: the obvious next step. You get there by doing the work earlier. Agreeing on the problem. Agreeing on the criteria. Agreeing on the value. If you've done that, the signature is a formality. If you haven't, no closing technique will save you. Make the whole conversation feel like the close. Then the close is just a signature.
C Demo Like Ice Cream
Show ↓
You don't hand someone a spoon and say: "Here's how ice cream is made." You give them a taste. Most product demos do the wrong thing. They explain. They feature-walk. They show everything. What they should do is give the customer the feeling of having the problem solved. Not "here's how it works." "Here's what your world looks like when it works." The most effective demos follow one rule: Lead with the outcome. Work backwards to the feature. "Imagine you start Monday morning and can see exactly which leads are ready to buy — this is where that shows up." That's a taste. Features close no one. The feeling of the problem already being solved — that closes deals.
C Know the Court
Show ↓
Before I walk into any high-stakes conversation, I ask myself: who's in the room? Not just names and titles. Who has authority? Who has influence with no authority? Who is silently opposed? Who wants this to work more than anyone? In basketball, the best players don't just see the ball. They see the court. In sales and leadership, the court is the room you're in. The politics. The dynamics. The history between the people. You can have the perfect pitch and still lose if you don't read the room. Prepare your content. But prepare your read even harder. Know who you're talking to. What they care about. What they're afraid of. Then walk in.
C I hate sales scripts
Show ↓
I hate sales scripts. Not because they don't work. But because they make you sound like you're reading one. And people can hear it. The alternative isn't to wing it. The alternative is to understand the principle behind the script so well that you don't need the script. Why do we open with rapport? Because people buy from people they like. Why do we start with their situation? Because people feel understood before they feel sold to. Why do we ask about consequences? Because pain is a more powerful motivator than gain. When you understand the why, you can have the same conversation a hundred different ways. And every version feels like it was made for that person. Because it was.
P You know things people would pay to learn
Show ↓
You know things other people would pay to learn. I'm certain of it. The problem is you can't see it from the inside. You've done something for so long that it feels obvious. Normal. Not worth charging for. That's the blind spot. What's obvious to you took you years to learn. Those years have value. The path to productizing your knowledge: Start with what you do naturally when someone asks for help. Notice the pattern in your advice. Name the framework. Test it with one person. Charge for the second. You don't need a perfect course or a polished offer. You need one person to pay you for what you already know how to do. Start there.
L Hire Slow. Fire Fast.
Show ↓
The most expensive decision I've made in leadership? Holding on too long. I knew within three weeks. The energy was wrong. The fit was wrong. But I told myself: they'll settle in. It takes time. Six months later, the team was exhausted and I was right where I started. Hiring slowly is a discipline. Use the interview to understand the person, not just the skills. What drives them when no one is watching? What broke them in their last role? How do they handle being wrong? Skills can be learned. Character can't be changed. And when you know it's not right — act. The kindest thing you can do for someone is give them the chance to find where they actually fit. Hire slow. But when you know, move fast.
L We don't have one target. We have three.
Show ↓
We don't have one target. We have three. Expected: The number that keeps the lights on. Good: The number that means we did something. GOAT: The number that means we actually went for it. Most teams set one number and call it a goal. The problem: once you hit it, the game is over. With three levels, someone who hits Expected doesn't feel like they won. And someone who misses GOAT but hits Good still has something to be proud of. Three levels also changes the conversation. "We hit Expected. What would have needed to be different to hit Good?" That's more useful than: "We hit the target. Great." Define your three levels. Then go after the one in the middle.
C The best sales conversations don't feel like sales
Show ↓
The best sales conversations don't feel like sales conversations. They feel like two people working together to figure out if there's a fit. That only happens when you're fully present. Not working through a checklist. Not waiting for the right moment to pivot to pricing. Actually listening. Most salespeople are thinking about their next move while the prospect is talking. The best ones are only thinking about what the prospect is saying right now. There's a word for this: intentionality. You can't be intentional and scripted at the same time. So instead of memorizing what to say, practice noticing what's actually happening. What is this person really telling you? What aren't they saying that matters? Show up with full attention. The rest takes care of itself.
P I work a full-time job. And I'm building something on the side.
Show ↓
I work a full-time job. And I'm building something on the side. I'm not going to pretend that's easy. There are evenings I'm too tired. Mornings I can't get started. Weeks where the side project feels pointless. But I keep coming back to one question: If not now, when? The moment is never perfect. The energy is never completely there. Something always needs attention first. The balance isn't about having equal time for both. It's about being intentional with the time you have. One focused hour beats three distracted ones. Build in the margins. Show up when you said you would. And remember why you started. The best time to begin was yesterday. The second best is today.
C Champion Building — You're never selling to a company
Show ↓
You're never selling to a company. You're selling to a person inside a company who wants to be the hero. That's your champion. They're the one who believed in the solution before everyone else did. They're the one who will walk into that internal meeting and fight for you when you're not in the room. Your job in enterprise sales isn't to close the deal. It's to make your champion look smart. Give them the language to explain your value. Give them the data to defend the decision. Give them the confidence to push through the internal friction. The most sophisticated salespeople I know don't sell. They build champions. Find yours. Arm them. Win together.
L The best salespeople care about more than their number
Show ↓
The best salespeople I've managed care about more than their number. They care about the company's number. There's a difference. A salesperson who only cares about their quota will close the wrong deals to hit it. A salesperson who cares about the company will close the right ones — and push back when something doesn't make sense. That's not idealism. That's aligned incentives. When you show your team the full picture — the revenue we need, why we need it, what it unlocks — something shifts. The pipeline stops being abstract. Every conversation has a reason. Share the context. Share the stakes. People rise to a mission. They grind through a quota.
P Self-care is not the reward. It's the infrastructure.
Show ↓
I'm not talking about bubble baths. I'm talking about the unsexy things that keep you sharp. Sleep. Movement. Food. The conversations you've been avoiding. Most high performers treat self-care as a reward. Something they'll do once the work is done. The work is never done. Self-care isn't the reward. It's the infrastructure. When I skip it, I notice it in two days. My conversations are shallower. My patience thinner. My decisions slower. When I'm consistent, everything is easier. Not because life gets easier. Because I'm sharper. Protect your energy with the same discipline you protect your calendar. It is a performance strategy.
P Values aren't what you believe. They're what you do when it costs you something.
Show ↓
I spent years chasing the wrong things. Not because I was stupid. Because I hadn't defined what mattered to me. Values aren't what you believe. They're what you do when it costs you something. Do you choose honesty when it's uncomfortable? That's a value. Do you protect your time even when people are disappointed? That's a value. Do you choose long-term over short-term consistently? That's a value. When you know your values, decisions get easier. Not painless. Easier. Because you have a filter. "Does this align with who I'm trying to be?" Yes or no. Know your values. Write them down. Then build your behavior around them — not the other way around.
P The Power of Lists
Show ↓
I'm a list person. I don't apologize for it. Lists do something that thinking alone doesn't. They externalize the chaos. When everything lives in your head, it competes for attention. The anxious thought. The creative idea. The forgotten task. All fighting at the same time. A list ends the competition. Here's what I've learned about making good lists: Don't filter while you write. Get it all out first. Review with intention. Not everything deserves action. One list for someday. One list for today. Never confuse the two. Cross things off slowly. Not because it's efficient — because the crossing-off is the reward. I've made decisions, solved problems, and calmed myself down with a piece of paper and a pen. The best thinking tool I've ever used costs nothing.
C The Three Pillars
Show ↓
After years in sales and leadership, I keep coming back to the same three pillars. They don't change. They don't get replaced by better ones. They just need to be practiced. 1. Know yourself. What drives you. What depletes you. Where you're strong and where you hide. The leader who knows themselves can be honest with their team. The salesperson who knows themselves doesn't need approval from every prospect. 2. Know your people. Not just their skills — their motivations. Their fears. What they need to perform. The best managers I've met don't manage tasks. They manage people. 3. Know the room. Read what's actually happening — not what should be happening. The energy. The resistance. The unspoken thing no one is saying. This is the skill that separates good from great. Self. People. Room. That's it. Everything else is a subset of one of these three.
C How to Close — without closing
Show ↓
People make closing harder than it is. They save it for the end. They treat it as a separate skill. They look for the magic phrase. But closing isn't what you do at the end. It's what you do throughout. Every time you confirm alignment: "So it sounds like this is important to you — is that right?" — you're closing. Every time you handle an objection early: "What would make this an easy yes for you?" — you're closing. Every time you establish criteria together: "If we could solve X and Y, would that be worth moving forward?" — you're closing. By the time you reach the end of a well-run conversation, the close is just a formality. The prospect has sold themselves. You just made sure they had what they needed to do it. The best close is the one that doesn't feel like a close. It feels like a natural next step.
L I ask about the weekend in every interview
Show ↓
Most interviews focus on what you've done. I want to know who you are when no one is watching. So I ask about the weekend. Not as small talk. As signal. How someone spends their time off tells you more about their character than their CV does. Do they have things they're building outside of work? Do they spend time with people they care about? Are they curious — do they read, explore, engage with ideas? Or do they coast until Monday? I'm not judging the answer. I'm looking for self-direction. The people who perform best in high-accountability environments aren't the ones waiting to be managed. They're the ones who already manage themselves. That shows up in how they talk about their life outside of work. Hire the whole person. Not just the résumé.
C The closing question I always come back to
Show ↓
After everything — the conversation, the discovery, the proposal — there's a question I always come back to. "Do you feel like I can solve your problem?" Simple. Direct. Human. Most salespeople close with urgency. "This offer expires Friday." "We only have two spots left." I close with honesty. If the answer is yes, I ask: "Why not now?" Not aggressively. Genuinely. Because if they believe I can solve their problem, the only thing standing between us and a decision is something practical — timing, budget, internal approval. And those are conversations I can have. If the answer is no — I want to know that too. Because then I either didn't communicate clearly, or I'm genuinely not the right fit. Both are important to understand. The best closes don't feel like closes. They feel like two people checking in on whether this makes sense. "Does this solve your problem?" Yes or no. Then we go from there.

C#9 · Session 9/9 · Don't ask if you're disturbing
