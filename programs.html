Research

Porges/Polyvagal Theory: the nervous system constantly scans for safety or threat — your presence is the first signal. Navarro: 93% of communication is non-verbal. Goleman: a leader's emotional state spreads neurobiologically — leader-to-follower synchrony is higher than peer-to-peer. HeartMath Institute: heart coherence measurably improves decision-making and cognitive clarity.

7 Energy Levels (ELI — Energy Leadership Index)

From Level 1 (victim energy — "life happens to me") to Level 7 (absolute passion — full creative flow). Most leaders operate at Level 2–3 under pressure. The goal isn't to always be at Level 7 — it's to choose your level deliberately and recognize when you've dropped.

2.1
The Polyvagal lens: safety, threat, and your nervous system

Your body scans for safety or threat before your conscious mind does. Learn to recognize when your system — and your team's — is in protection mode versus connection mode.

2.2
The 7 energy levels — where are you operating from?

Catabolic vs anabolic energy. Where you and your team operate under pressure — and how to shift the room upward intentionally.

2.3
Read the room before you speak

Body language, micro-expressions, tone, what isn't said. What Joe Navarro (FBI) calls the "honest body" — the signals people give without knowing it.

2.4
Your energy is contagious — always

Neurological synchrony and why it matters. A leader's emotional state spreads to the team faster than peer-to-peer. Your regulation is not optional — it's your first leadership act.

2.5
HeartMath: heart coherence as a leadership tool

When your heart and nervous system are in coherence, your perception sharpens and your decisions improve. The Inner Balance practice in 2 minutes.

2.6
Energy as your compass — trust what you sense

The leader who knows when to bring energy and when to hold back changes the room without saying a word. Tony Schwartz: energy management is more important than time management.

E — Exploration: The Full Conversation
Current → Dream → Problem → Probe · Logical + Emotional Certainty
▼

Learning objectives

Qualify and disqualify fast — respect both parties' time
Set a clear agenda and map dream → current state → problem → probe
Build both logical AND emotional certainty
Use loss aversion deliberately and ethically
Let the customer find their own way to needing a solution

Research

95% of purchase decisions happen unconsciously (HBS). Loss aversion is 2x more powerful than gain. Only 1 in 20 questions are "implication questions" — the highest-impact ones.

3.1
Disqualify Fast — respect both parties' time

Not everyone is the right customer. The faster you find out, the better for both parties. Disqualify Fast happens on the first call — before you invest time in meetings.

3.2
Agenda Setting — you set the frame, not the customer

Set the frame from the start. "What would you like to get out of this meeting?" You steer the direction. A clear agenda creates safety and focus for both parties.

3.3
Start with the dream — always

Open position, not defensive. Dream → Current State → Problem. Always in that order. The order changes everything.

3.4
Current State — map the reality actively

Where are they now? What are they using today? What works — what doesn't? Current State creates the gap to the dream and makes the problem concrete.

3.5
"Tell me a little more" — the question that opens everything

The most powerful question in sales. Opens rather than closes. Customers rarely say the real problem out loud — hear what lies beneath.

3.6
Probe: widen the gap between current state and the dream

Not manipulation — full understanding. When the customer fully feels the gap, they start looking for a way across. You're that bridge.

3.7
Logical certainty: cause, problem and gap

Find the gap. Map it precisely. The customer needs to understand logically why staying where they are costs more than moving forward.

3.8
Emotional certainty: visualise the future + identity shift

We don't buy products — we buy better versions of ourselves. What does life look like on the other side? How does it feel personally?

3.9
Flip side: what happens if they DON'T act?

Loss aversion is twice as powerful as gain. "What happens in 2 days, 2 weeks, 2 months, 2 years if nothing changes?"

3.10
Fuck Yes or Fuck No — set the frame early

Yes or no are both fine — "maybe" serves no one. This removes pressure, creates honesty, and gives the customer psychological safety to be direct. It's not a close — it's an exploration frame.

Learning objectives

Use Negative Reverse Selling to open conversations and book meetings
Set the expectation of a clear yes or no — avoid maybe
Present the solution as an extension of the customer's own words

Understand that L in sales = liberating the customer from indecision — not closing them

Research

The pitch phase is not persuasion — it's confirmation. The customer decided emotionally in Elevate. Keep it to 3–5 minutes.

Negative Reverse Selling — Power of No

You formulate the question as a negative — because "no" is psychologically safe to say.

Most prospects won't say "yes" to something they're not 100% sure about. But they'll say "no, it's not out of the question" — because it commits them to nothing. That's your opening. The technique works because you remove the pressure. You're not asking if they WILL — you're asking if it's UNTHINKABLE that they would.

Example — book a meeting

You:

"Would it be completely out of the question for us to meet on Friday?"

Not: "Do you want to meet?" or "Are you interested?"

Prospect:

"No..."

No = "no, it's not out of the question" = yes, let's meet

You:

"Great — what works best, morning or afternoon?"

Confirm and move forward. Don't discuss the no.

4.1
Negative Reverse Selling — Power of No

You formulate the question as a negation so the prospect says no — but no means "no, it's not out of the question" = yes. No is psychologically safer than yes. "Would it be completely out of the question for us to meet on Friday?" — "No." — "Great, morning or afternoon?"

4.2
Negative Reverse in practice — when do you use it?

Booking meetings, testing commitment, reopening cold leads, closing conversations that have stalled. Always when you sense the prospect pulling back.

4.3
3-Pillar Pitching

Gather the customer's own words and present three solutions directly connected to what they said. "Based on what you told me..." Context is trust. Nothing generic — everything is an answer to what the customer said. Keep it to 3–5 minutes.

4.4
Pre-close: the customer sells to themselves

"Do you feel I can solve your problem? Yes? Why?" They convince themselves. You don't close — you confirm.

4.5
Champion building and lasting relationships

Identify the person internally who believes in you most and help them sell internally. Book onboarding before the negotiation is finished. Customers who feel seen and understood stay.
