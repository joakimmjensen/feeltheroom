Hire Interview Guide

The whole-person interview framework for hiring salespeople who perform — and stay.
The Philosophy Most interviews hire for the résumé and fire for the person. This guide does the opposite — it treats the candidate as a whole human being: their stories, their self-image, their blind spots, and the gap between who they think they are and who they actually are. Skills can be trained. Character and self-awareness cannot.
Before Before the Interview
  • Review their written application for energy and specificity — not just credentials
  • Prepare 3 things you want to understand about them as a person, not just as a candidate
  • Set your own state: you are also being evaluated
Section 1 Opening First 5 minutes
Goal: make them feel safe enough to be honest
"Tell me a bit about where you are right now — not your CV, just where you're at."
"What made you say yes to this conversation?"
What to observe Do they relax into the conversation or stay "on"? High-Dominance candidates get to the point fast and take up space. High-Patience candidates warm up slowly and are more deliberate. Notice their language: visual ("I see", "it looks like"), auditory ("sounds right", "I hear you"), or kinesthetic ("feels off", "gut sense") — this reveals how they process the world.
Section 1b The Mirror Moment 5 minutes
Goal: surface the gap between self-image and how they're actually perceived
"If I called your two best colleagues from your last role and asked them to describe you in three words — what do you think they'd say?"
Listen for: specificity and honesty. Vague answers ("hardworking, team player") suggest low self-awareness. Specific answers reveal someone who has actually thought about how they land on others.
"And if I asked your two closest friends — the ones who know you outside of work — what would they say?"
Listen for: is the professional self and the personal self the same person? Or two very different answers? Consistency signals integration. A big gap can signal a persona being performed at work.
"Now — does that match how you see yourself? Where's the gap?"
Listen for: can they name the gap with curiosity rather than defence? This is the Peter Crone moment — the story beneath the story. The candidate who says "yeah, they'd probably say I'm hard to read and I think that's true" is more valuable than the one who says "I think we'd fully agree."
What to observe Watch what happens in their body when they move from "what colleagues would say" to "what friends would say." A shift in posture, pace, or tone often signals where the real person lives. If the two answers are almost identical, they may not have thought about it — or they may be managing the impression they're making on you right now.
Section 2 Behavioural Questions 20 minutes
"Tell me about the best sale you ever made. Walk me through exactly what happened."
Listen for: awareness of the customer's state, not just the technique
"Tell me about a time you lost a deal you thought you had. What happened — and what did you take from it?"
Listen for: self-awareness, ownership, not blame
"Describe your best manager. What did they do that others didn't?"
Listen for: what kind of leadership they respond to
"When you're having a bad week, what do you do?"
Listen for: emotional regulation, self-management
"What do you do outside of work that you're genuinely proud of?"
Listen for: identity beyond the job — the whole person
"If you're being completely honest with yourself — what's the one thing you think has held you back the most?"
Listen for: can they sit in the question without rushing? Do they own it, or do they redirect to circumstances? Self-awareness at identity level — not just behaviour. (Peter Crone: the story beneath the story.)
"What's the last important thing that didn't go as planned or as you wanted? And what do you most honestly believe was the reason?"
Listen for: do they own the outcome or explain it away? The answer reveals their relationship with accountability more than any other question. Let them sit in the silence after they answer — the follow-up they give themselves is often more telling than the first response.
"How comfortable are you with the no? Walk me through what happens inside you when a customer — or a conversation — doesn't go the way you hoped."
Listen for: do they accept the no, learn from it, and move on — or do they carry it? The best salespeople aren't fearless. They're comfortable with rejection because they don't take it personally. A candidate who says "it motivates me" without any nuance is performing. A candidate who says "it stings for a moment, but I've learned to separate the no from my worth" is telling the truth.
What to observe Who do they put at the centre of their stories — themselves or others? Watch for energy shifts: do they light up on wins and go flat on losses, or stay emotionally consistent? The candidate who can describe a failure with curiosity instead of defence is rare — and right.
Section 3 Culture & Energy Questions 10 minutes
Goal: understand their natural environment — where they thrive vs. struggle
"What kind of team brings out the best in you?"
"What do you need from a leader to perform at your best?"
"Is there anything about this role or company that you're unsure about?"
A good candidate has real questions. No questions = no curiosity.
"What are you actively working on improving about yourself right now — as a person, not just a professional?"
Listen for: is personal growth a lifestyle or a reaction to feedback? The best people are always working on something. Specificity matters. (Jim Rohn: work harder on yourself than on your job.)
"What's the last thing you read, listened to, or learned that actually changed how you think?"
Listen for: curiosity and intentional inputs. Who they learn from says a lot about who they are becoming. (Jim Rohn: you are the average of the five people you spend the most time with — and the ideas you let in.)
"How would you describe your inner circle?"
Listen for: do they describe people who challenge and push them — or people who are simply comfortable and familiar? The best performers are intentional about who they surround themselves with. Vague answers ("good friends, supportive people") often signal someone who hasn't thought about this. (Jim Rohn: you become who you spend time with.)
What to observe Are they naturally matching your pace and energy? Rapport without effort is a strong signal of social awareness. Notice if their answers become more open and specific as the conversation deepens — or if they stay surface-level throughout. The best candidates reveal more of themselves as the conversation feels safer.
Closing The Final Question
Goal: see how they think when there's no right answer
"We're near the end. If you could ask me one question right now — the question you think would matter most after a conversation like this — what would it be?"
Listen for: do they ask something bold and specific, or do they default to something safe ("What are the next steps?")? The best candidates ask something that reveals they've been thinking — not just answering. A great question here is itself a demonstration of the thing you've been evaluating all along: curiosity, self-awareness, and the ability to read the room.
What to observe Do they pause before asking — or do they have something ready? A pause usually means they're thinking honestly. Watch their energy here: are they genuinely curious, or are they trying to impress you? The best closing question is one that surprises you.
Section 4 Red Flags
  • Blames previous employers or customers for failures
  • Can't describe a loss with any self-reflection
  • Has no answer to "what brings out the best in you"
  • Only talks about themselves — never about the customer or the team
  • No genuine curiosity about the role or company
