Objection Mapping Framework

The 6 patterns covering 74% of all objections. What they really mean — and how to respond.
An objection is not a no. It's a question wrapped in caution. Most objections aren't about the price, the timing, or the decision-maker — they're about one thing: the customer doesn't feel certain enough yet.
How to read objections

Most objections aren't logical — they're stories. "It's too expensive" is often a story about scarcity. "The timing isn't right" is a story about priorities. "I need to think about it" is a story about risk. Your job is not to argue with the story — it's to get curious about what's underneath it.

Section 0 The Door Principle

Before you respond — diagnose first. Every objection is a door, not a wall.

Logistics door

Practical — fixable

Money. Time. Decision authority. Real constraints — but workable if the desire is there.

Fear door

Emotional — needs certainty

Uncertainty. Risk. "What if this doesn't work for me?" Logic doesn't open this door. Certainty does.

Ask yourself first: Is what they're saying a practical constraint — or a comfortable way to say they're not convinced yet?

Logistics objections need solutions. Fear objections need certainty. Treating a fear door like a logistics door is the most common mistake in sales.
Section 1 The LAER Technique

Your standard move for any objection.

L Listen
Let them finish. Don't interrupt. Don't prepare your answer while they talk.
A Acknowledge
"That makes complete sense." / "I hear you." Never argue.
E Explore
"Tell me a little more about that." / "What specifically is making you hesitant?"
R Respond
Only after you understand. Then respond — with their words, not yours.
Section 2 The 6 Patterns
Pattern 1 "It's too expensive" / "The price is too high"
Real meaning I don't see enough value yet.
The mistake Defending the price or offering a discount immediately.
LAER Response
A "I hear you — price is always a real consideration."
E "Help me understand — too expensive compared to what?"
R "What would it cost you if this problem stays unsolved?" → back to Elevate
Neutralize the constraint
"Setting the money aside for a second — if cost wasn't the issue, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"
Yes → now you're both solving the constraint together. No → there's a fear door underneath.
Prevention If the Probe phase was done well, they already see the cost of inaction. Price feels small against that.
Pattern 2 "I need to think about it"
Real meaning I'm not emotionally convinced yet. Something is unresolved.
The mistake Saying "of course, take your time" and following up in a week.
LAER Response
A "Absolutely — this is a real decision."
E "What specifically will you be thinking about? I want to make sure you have everything you need."
R Address whatever they name. If they can't name it — the real objection is elsewhere. "We said at the start we'd land on a clear yes or no — what would need to be true for this to be a yes?"
Neutralize the constraint
"Setting the timing aside — if that wasn't in the picture, is this something you'd want to do?"
Yes → the thinking is really about fear, not time. That's where to go next. No → there's something else unresolved.
Prevention The upfront yes/no agreement (Step 1) makes "maybe" harder to land on. The pre-close (Step 7) surfaces uncertainty before it becomes a stall.
Pattern 3 "I need to talk to my manager / partner / board"
Real meaning I'm not confident enough to champion this internally.
The mistake Saying "okay, let me know what they say."
LAER Response
A "That makes sense — this involves more than just you."
E "What do you think they'll want to understand? What's their main concern likely to be?"
R Help them sell it internally. "Would it be useful if I put something together for that conversation?"
Neutralize the constraint
"Setting your manager aside for a moment — if it was purely your decision, would you move forward?"
Yes → they're your champion. Help them win the internal conversation. No → they're not sold yet, and the manager is a comfortable exit.
Prevention Champion building — identify early who else is involved and bring them in.
Pattern 4 "We already use [competitor]"
Real meaning Switching feels risky and effortful. Is it worth it?
The mistake Criticizing the competitor or listing your features.
LAER Response
A "That's good to know — you've clearly already invested in this."
E "What do you like about how it's working? And what would you change if you could?"
R Find the gap. You're not selling against the competitor — you're selling against the gap.
Prevention Good Current State mapping surfaces what's not working before the pitch.
Pattern 5 "The timing isn't right" / "Maybe next quarter"
Real meaning This isn't a high enough priority yet.
The mistake Agreeing and scheduling a follow-up 3 months out.
LAER Response
A "Timing matters — I get it."
E "Help me understand — what's driving that? What's taking priority right now?"
R "What happens if you wait 6 months? In time, money, team energy?" → loss aversion
Neutralize the constraint
"Setting the timing aside — if right now was actually a good time, is this something you'd want to do?"
Yes → timing is logistics. Solve it together. No → this is a fear door, and urgency won't help.
Prevention Urgency must be built in Probe — not created at Close. If timing is chronic, disqualify fast: a no today is better than a maybe for 3 months.
Pattern 6 "I'm not sure it'll work for us" / "We're different"
Real meaning I don't believe the promise applies to me specifically.
The mistake Listing more features or quoting generic case studies.
LAER Response
A "That's a fair concern — not everything fits every situation."
E "What specifically makes you think your situation is different? Tell me more."
R Use their words to show how your solution addresses their specific context — not a general claim. "That's exactly the situation I'm thinking about when I say..."
Prevention Deep Current State and Dream mapping makes this almost impossible — they see themselves in the solution before you pitch it.

Section 4 The Consequence Close

When LAER hasn't moved them — and you've earned the right to go deeper.

The Crown

"Who is responsible for putting yourself in the best position possible?"

Not a challenge — a genuine question. When they own the answer, they stop being a passenger and start being a decision-maker. The Crown shifts responsibility back to them — not to pressure, but to help them lead.

1
Frame

"Here's what I think is true for you right now..." — name their situation in their words. Show that you see them clearly.

2
Push back

"I hear you saying [objection]. Help me understand that a little more." — don't accept the surface. Go one level deeper.

3
Consequence

"If this stays the same — what does next year look like for you?" — make the cost of inaction real and personal. Not a threat. A mirror.

4
CTA

"So given all of that — what would you like to do?" — quiet. Simple. Let the silence work.

This is not a pressure technique. It's a clarity technique. You're helping them see what they already know — and giving them the space to act on it.
The one rule
"The best time to handle an objection is before it arrives. If your F, E, and E are done well — most objections disappear on their own."
And when they do arrive: get curious, not defensive. An objection is not an attack — it's the customer telling you what they still need to believe.
