4 Elicitation Techniques

How to get people to reveal information they normally wouldn't share

01
Leading Statements

Make a statement (not a question) that invites a response. They feel understood, not interrogated — and reveal more than if asked directly.

02
Flattery + Read the Deflection

Give a compliment and watch how they deflect. The deflection reveals background, self-image, and values. ("Oh no, I was just lucky because...").

03
Complaint Baiting

Casually mention a relatable frustration. Complaining feels freeing — people over-share and open up about the real issue.

04
Speech Mirroring

Repeat the last 3 words of what they said. They feel heard, and naturally continue talking — revealing more and more.

Stress & Deception Signals

You can't detect lies directly — but you can detect stress. Stress = something is off

Blink Rate
Normal = ~9x/min. Sudden increase = stress. Slow down = comfort & relaxation.
Finger Closing
Subtle inward curl of fingers = stress. Extended, open fingers = ease.
Facial Touching
Touching face or covering mouth = instinctive stress response (universal).
Hesitation
Long pause before answering, or repeating the question back = buying time to construct a story.
Speed & Pitch
Voice gets higher and faster = stress / possible deception. Watch for change from baseline.
Caveats
"As far as I know..." / "If I remember correctly..." = omitting something on purpose.

F.E.E.L. for Leaders

Marcus manages 3-10 B2B reps. These are his Chase Hughes moments.

Know Your Rep's 2 Social Needs

Before your 1:1, profile your rep. Do they need approval (praise them = they perform) or importance (give them ownership = they go all in)? Stop managing everyone the same way.

Elicitation in Performance Reviews

Don't ask "Are you struggling?" — use complaint baiting: "I imagine it's been tough to keep momentum with these cold call volumes." They'll tell you the real problem.

Detect Low Performance Before It Happens

Watch for stress signals in your reps: sudden closed body language, increased facial touching in team meetings, voice pitch rising when asked about their pipeline. Catch it early.

Hiring: Read Decision-Making Style

In interviews, listen for the candidate's decision driver. "Conformity" reps do well in structured teams. "Deviance" reps need autonomy. Mismatch = turnover.

Authority Model for Leaders

Hughes' 5 pillars: Confidence, Discipline, Leadership, Gratitude, Enjoyment. Your team follows you more when they sense all five — not just results and pressure.

Speech Mirroring in Team Conversations

In 1:1s, mirror your rep's last 3 words. They feel heard. They open up. You discover the real blocker — not the surface excuse.

F.E.E.L. for Sales

The B2B sales rep. These are Chase Hughes skills that close deals.

Profile the Buyer in 6 Minutes

Before your pitch, identify their 2 social needs. Buyer who talks about their team's trust in them = needs to feel important. Frame your offer around what makes them look like the smart one.

Match Their Decision-Making Style

"We have a lot of your competitors already using this" = conformity driver. "This is something most companies haven't adopted yet" = novelty/deviance driver. Same product, different frame.

Leading Statements in Discovery

Instead of "What's your biggest challenge?" say: "I imagine with a team your size, follow-up consistency is probably a constant battle." They confirm, deny, or reveal the real issue — no interrogation feeling.

Fear-Based Closing (Ethical)

Once you know their fear (e.g. fear of being seen as unintelligent), tell a story of a buyer who passed and made a costly mistake. You don't threaten — you imply. They close themselves.

Detect Objections Before They're Said

Increased blink rate when you mention price? Finger curl when you name a competitor? These are buying signals in reverse — surface the real objection before the call ends.

Flattery to Get Intel

Compliment their operation. The deflection reveals who the real decision-maker is, what budget looks like, and how much urgency exists. "You've clearly built a strong team" → "Oh, we try — honestly though, our manager just gave us a new quota…"
